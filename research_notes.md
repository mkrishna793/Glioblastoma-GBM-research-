# 🧬 Glioblastoma Multiforme (GBM): Research Baseline & Biological Notes

This document establishes the clinical and biological baseline for our Glioblastoma Multiforme (GBM) genomic research, refining the core characteristics of the tumor.

---

## 📌 The 5 Core Characteristics of GBM

### 1. Genetic Mutations & Triggers
* **Your Understanding**: Caused by mutations, but the exact trigger for why these mutations start is mostly unknown.
* **Biological Context**: **Accurate.** GBM is driven by somatic (acquired) mutations in key regulatory pathways (e.g., RTK/RAS/PI3K, p53, Rb). While ionizing radiation is a known environmental trigger, and rare genetic syndromes (like Li-Fraumeni) increase risk, the vast majority of cases occur spontaneously with no identifiable external trigger.

### 2. Malignant & Invasive Growth
* **Your Understanding**: It is a malignant tumor that moves and grows very fast.
* **Biological Context**: **Accurate, with a crucial nuance.** GBM is highly proliferative (grows rapidly) and highly infiltrative (invades surrounding tissue). However, unlike other malignant cancers, **GBM almost never metastasizes (spreads) outside the brain and spinal cord**. It "moves" by invading locally, using white matter tracts as pathways to migrate to other regions of the brain.

### 3. Spatial Heterogeneity (The "Subtype Map")
* **Your Understanding**: A scan can show different variations of the tumor in different locations, like a mesenchymal core and a proneural outer edge.
* **Biological Context**: **Accurate.** This is called **intratumoral heterogeneity**. GBM tumors are divided into transcriptional subtypes: **Proneural, Classical, and Mesenchymal**. A single GBM tumor is not uniform; the hypoxic/necrotic core of the tumor often exhibits a *Mesenchymal* state, while the active, invading outer edge often resembles a *Proneural* or *Classical* state.

### 4. Therapeutic Plasticity (Subtype Switching)
* **Your Understanding**: Chemotherapy and radiation change the environment, causing the tumor to adapt, mutate, and switch subtypes (like Proneural-to-Mesenchymal).
* **Biological Context**: **Accurate.** Under the selective pressure of treatments like Temozolomide (TMZ) and radiation, the tumor adapts. A classic mechanism is **Proneural-to-Mesenchymal Transition (PMT)**, where the tumor switches to a mesenchymal state, which is much more resistant to therapy. This is driven by both new mutations (clonal evolution) and epigenetic adaptation (plasticity).

### 5. Neuronal Damage & Excitotoxicity
* **Your Understanding**: The tumor doesn't just grow; it actively damages normal neurons.
* **Biological Context**: **Accurate.** The tumor destroys neurons through:
  * **Physical Compression**: Increased pressure within the skull.
  * **Ischemia**: Starving normal brain cells of oxygen by hijacking the blood supply (angiogenesis).
  * **Excitotoxicity**: GBM cells actively secrete high levels of **glutamate** (a neurotransmitter). Excessive glutamate overstimulates surrounding normal neurons, causing them to die, which clears physical space for the tumor to expand.
